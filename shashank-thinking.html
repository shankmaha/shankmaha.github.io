Program Management

Why 90% On-Time Delivery Starts Before the Kickoff

Shank Mahadev 8 min read

Every program I've ever seen blow up — and I've seen a few — followed the same pattern. The kickoff meeting was great. Energy was high, slides were polished, someone had made a Confluence page with an ambitious Gantt chart that looked like it had been drawn by an optimist on their best day. Then, about six weeks in, reality arrived. Uninvited, as it always does.

And here's the thing nobody wants to say out loud in that first debrief: the program was already dead at the kickoff meeting. It just didn't know it yet.

I learned this the hard way running platform programs across six countries at once. You cannot sprint your way out of a dependency problem you didn't map before you started. You cannot retrospective your way out of a missed API integration that three teams needed and nobody owned. The sprint review is too late. The kickoff is too late. The decisions that actually determine delivery outcome happen in the unglamorous weeks before any of that.

The real pre-work nobody does

Ask any delivery lead what they did before kickoff and you'll hear about project charters, RACI matrices, maybe a risk register that was lovingly constructed and never looked at again. What you won't hear is the honest answer: I talked to every team that touches this program and asked them what would make them miss their date.

That conversation — uncomfortable, time-consuming, and wildly unpopular with people who want to "stay positive" — is where delivery is actually won or lost.

At Flight Centre, running a $4M platform transformation across ERP, Salesforce, and a dozen integrations, I spent three weeks before kickoff doing nothing but dependency mapping. Not the sanitised version in a project plan. The real one. Who needs what from whom, by when, and what breaks if that's late by two weeks? What external vendors are involved? What does their roadmap look like and who owns that relationship? Where are the single points of failure — the one engineer who knows how the IAM system works and is also taking leave in week eight?

"The sprint review is too late. The kickoff is too late. The unglamorous weeks before any of that — that's where delivery is won."

Three weeks of that felt like overkill to everyone around me. Then the program ran at 90% on-time across 27 engineers and six regions. Nobody complained about the three weeks after that.

What dependency mapping actually means

It doesn't mean drawing boxes and arrows in Miro for three hours and calling it done. It means answering five questions for every workstream:

  • What does this workstream need from another team to start? Not eventually — to actually start.
  • What does another team need from this workstream, and when? Make it specific. "Sometime in Q2" is not a date.
  • What is the single thing that, if delayed, delays everything downstream? That thing needs an owner, a contingency, and your personal attention.
  • Where are the vendor or third-party dependencies? External teams don't care about your sprint cadence.
  • Who is irreplaceable, and what's the plan if they're not available? This question makes everyone uncomfortable. Ask it anyway.

The Gantt chart is lying to you

I have a complicated relationship with Gantt charts. They're beautiful. They're satisfying. They look like someone has things under control. They are also almost always a work of fiction produced by someone who was told to make a timeline and did their best under considerable optimism.

The issue isn't the tool. It's that Gantt charts show you the happy path. They show tasks in sequence, each one completing cleanly before the next begins, like a very organised set of dominoes. Real programs don't work like that. Real programs have five things happening in parallel, each one blocked on a different team, all of them due in the same two-week window, with one engineer shared across three of them.

When I present a delivery plan now, I present two versions. The plan, and the risk-adjusted plan. The risk-adjusted plan assumes the three things most likely to go wrong actually go wrong. It shows what happens to the timeline. It's uncomfortable to show stakeholders. It's also the one that has saved me from every "how did nobody see this coming" conversation I might otherwise have had.

Start earlier than feels necessary

The single most impactful thing I've done consistently across every program that delivered on time is start the pre-work earlier than felt remotely necessary. Not two weeks before kickoff. Six weeks. Eight weeks for anything genuinely complex.

People look at you funny. They ask what you could possibly be doing that far out. The answer is: the thing that will determine whether this program succeeds or fails. You're just doing it quietly, before anyone is watching, which is exactly when it's most effective.

The kickoff meeting is not the start of delivery. It's the announcement that delivery has already begun.

Engineering Leadership

4 to 27: What Scaling a Team Actually Looks Like

Shank Mahadev 9 min read

When I joined the Envoyage division at Flight Centre, the engineering team had four people. Talented, stretched, mildly terrified. By the time I left two years later, it was twenty-seven. And the thing I most want to tell anyone staring down a similar mandate is this: the hiring is the easy part.

I know that sounds insane. Hiring is famously brutal. Sourcing, interviewing, negotiating, onboarding — it's exhausting. But compared to the real problem — which is building the operating model that makes each new person a force multiplier rather than a coordination tax — hiring is a pleasant afternoon.

Most teams scale headcount. They don't scale the system. The result is a team that's technically larger and functionally slower. I've watched it happen. I've felt it happen. Here's what I learned from making it work the other way.

The first ten are the foundation. Don't rush them.

There's enormous pressure, when you're scaling fast, to hire quickly. Get bodies in seats. Fill the roles. The business needs more capacity — now. I understand that pressure. I've felt it from every direction simultaneously.

But the first ten people you bring into a scaling team set the culture, the standards, and the operating norms for everyone who comes after them. If you hire fast and loose in that window, you're not building a foundation — you're building a problem that compounds every time someone new joins.

"Each new hire either raises the bar or lowers it. There is no neutral hire."

I hired slowly for the first eight people. Slower than anyone wanted. I was looking for technical competence, obviously — but more than that I was looking for people who would set a standard. Who would push back when something was wrong. Who would take ownership without being asked. Each of those eight people eventually became the person a new joiner watched to understand how things worked here. That's not a small thing to get right.

The operating model is the product

When you go from four to ten engineers, you can still run things on feel. Everyone knows what's happening, communication happens naturally, you can hold the whole picture in your head. At fifteen, that stops working. By twenty, running on feel is a disaster.

What you need — and what almost nobody builds early enough — is an operating model. Not a fancy term. Just the answer to: how do decisions get made here? How does work get prioritised? How do teams coordinate when they depend on each other? How does a new person know what good looks like?

I built this in parallel with hiring, not after. Sprint structures, OKR rhythms, dependency forums, onboarding playbooks, architecture review cadences. Boring? Completely. Essential? Without question. Every hour I spent building that scaffolding saved ten hours of confusion, duplicated effort, and "wait, who owns this?" conversations at the worst possible time.

Attrition will tell you everything

One number I track obsessively on any team I lead: attrition. Not because I'm sentimental about it, but because it is the most honest measure of whether your operating model is working. Happy, challenged, well-directed engineers don't leave. Engineers who feel like they're spinning in circles, whose work doesn't matter, who can't see a path forward — they leave. And they leave at the worst possible time, taking critical knowledge with them.

We maintained 0% attrition through the most intense delivery period — scaling to 27 people across APAC and EMEA simultaneously, running multiple platform programs at once. That didn't happen by accident. It happened because I treated retention as a delivery risk, not an HR metric. Every six weeks I had a direct conversation with every person on my team about what was working, what wasn't, and what they needed to feel like they were growing. Uncomfortable? Sometimes. Worth it? Every time.

The moment you realise you've scaled

There's a moment — and you'll know it when it happens — when you realise the team is running without you holding it together. A decision gets made correctly without you in the room. A new person gets onboarded smoothly without you shepherding them. Two teams resolve a dependency conflict between themselves without escalating it to you.

That's the moment. That's what you were building towards. It feels slightly like being made redundant and completely like success, simultaneously.

Scaling a team is not a hiring project. It's a systems design project where the system is made of people. Treat it accordingly.

AI / GenAI

We Piloted AWS Bedrock in Production. Here's What Worked.

Shank Mahadev 10 min read

Everyone wants to talk about AI. Executives want to talk about AI. Investors want to talk about AI. The person next to you on a flight wants to talk about AI. What almost nobody wants to talk about is the part where you actually try to ship something with it and discover that the gap between the demo and production is approximately the size of the Grand Canyon.

We piloted AWS Bedrock — specifically Claude and Titan — at Flight Centre's Envoyage division, building traveller insights and operational analytics pipelines. It worked. It actually delivered ROI. But the path to "it worked" had some detours I wasn't entirely expecting, and I think those detours are more useful to share than the success story.

Why we did it

The honest origin story: we had a massive volume of traveller data — booking patterns, preference signals, operational events — and a small team trying to extract insights from it manually. Analysts were spending 60-70% of their time wrangling data rather than doing anything useful with it. That's not an AI problem. That's a "we are wasting expensive human time on something a well-configured system could handle" problem. AI was one potential answer.

We picked AWS Bedrock partly because we were already on AWS infrastructure, and partly because managed model access meant we weren't also spinning up the ML infrastructure from scratch. The right tool for the right context. Not because it was the shiniest thing in the room.

What actually worked

Summarisation and pattern extraction at scale. The thing that delivered the most obvious ROI, fastest. Taking large volumes of traveller feedback and booking data and surfacing structured summaries — themes, anomalies, patterns — that used to take a human analyst a day now happened in minutes. This was the first thing we put in front of stakeholders and the first thing that made someone say "okay, I see it now."

Operational query answering. Building an interface where operations staff could ask plain-language questions about data — "show me all bookings for corporate accounts in ANZ that had service issues in the last 30 days" — and get coherent answers without writing SQL. Adoption was faster than anything I've seen rolled out to operational teams, because it actually made their jobs easier rather than adding a new system to learn.

The data readiness work we did beforehand. This isn't glamorous but it's the real answer. Before a single model was called, we spent six weeks cleaning, structuring, and documenting the data. Teams that skip this step get garbage outputs and conclude AI doesn't work. The model is only as good as what you feed it.

What didn't work

Generative outputs in customer-facing contexts, without a human in the loop, faster than we were comfortable with. Every time someone proposed using AI to generate something a customer would see directly — an email, a personalised recommendation, a response to a query — the governance questions got complicated quickly. Not impossible. Just slower than the enthusiasm suggested.

Overambitious scope in the first sprint. The first version of what we wanted to build was, in retrospect, approximately eighteen months of work dressed up in a six-week timeline by people who had been watching too many conference keynotes. We had to cut it to something shippable, prove the value, and then expand. Classic mistake, made by people who absolutely knew better.

"The model is only as good as what you feed it. The six weeks we spent on data readiness before touching a model is the reason it worked."

What I'd do differently

Start with a smaller, more specific problem than feels necessary. The instinct is to demonstrate broad capability. The smarter move is to solve one painful, specific problem beautifully, get stakeholders hooked, then expand. A GenAI initiative that solves one real problem compellingly is worth infinitely more than a broad platform nobody uses.

Get a commercial owner on the initiative from day one. The technical team can build it. The program team can deliver it. But if there isn't someone with a P&L who can articulate why this matters in business terms — cost saved, revenue enabled, risk reduced — it will not survive the first budget conversation.

AI is not magic. It is a genuinely powerful set of tools that requires the same delivery rigour, stakeholder management, data discipline, and change management as any other program. The teams that treat it as magic are the ones who end up with expensive experiments that never make it to production. The teams that treat it as engineering — with real governance, real data foundations, real user research — are the ones who actually ship.

The Governance Trap: When Process Kills the Program

Shank Mahadev 8 min read

I want to tell you about a steering committee meeting I once sat in. It was the seventh consecutive fortnightly steering committee for a program that had been in flight for four months. By my count, the program had spent approximately 140 collective human hours in status meetings. It had spent approximately zero hours resolving the single blocker that had been on the risk register since week two.

The blocker, by the way, was a vendor contract that needed one signature from one executive who was in every single one of those steering committee meetings. Nobody had asked him to sign it. We were too busy updating the RAG status from amber to red and back to amber again.

This is the governance trap. And it is absolutely everywhere.

How governance gets invented

Governance doesn't start bad. It starts with genuine, reasonable intention. Something goes wrong on a program. A decision gets made without the right people in the room. A risk goes unnoticed until it's a crisis. Leadership, reasonably, decides that some structure is needed to prevent this happening again.

So a governance framework gets created. A steering committee. A weekly status report. A risk register. A RAID log. An escalation matrix. An architecture review board. A change control process. A dependency forum. A delivery assurance meeting to review the output of the delivery meeting.

Each of these things was invented to solve a real problem. By the time they're all running simultaneously, they have become the problem.

"The risk register had 47 items. Thirty-one of them were risks to the risk register process itself. I wish I was joking."

The tell-tale signs

You're in the governance trap when:

  • More time is spent preparing status updates than resolving the issues in them.
  • Engineers are attending governance meetings instead of writing code.
  • A decision that should take one conversation takes three meetings and a written proposal.
  • The risk register is so long that nobody reads it, which means nobody manages the risks.
  • The phrase "we'll need to take that through the proper process" is used to avoid making a decision.
  • There is a meeting to decide who should attend the meeting about the meeting.

I'm laughing as I write this. Faintly. Because I have lived every single one of these.

What governance is actually for

Good governance has one job: make decisions at the right level, at the right speed, with the right information. That's it. Every governance structure should be evaluated against that single test. Does this forum make decisions? Does it make them quickly enough? Does it have the information it needs?

If the answer to any of those is no, the forum is not governance. It is a very expensive performance of governance.

The best governance I've run is boring. A fifteen-minute weekly call for escalations only — if there's nothing to escalate, the call doesn't happen. A monthly steering committee with three agenda items maximum: decisions needed, risks requiring executive action, forward visibility on the next six weeks. Nothing else. No status theatre. If you want to know the status, read the dashboard.

The hardest conversation to have

The governance trap is politically difficult to dismantle because the people inside it often have their identity tied to running it. The governance framework becomes their job. Telling someone that their elaborate status reporting system is preventing delivery feels personal, even when it's just true.

I've had this conversation. Multiple times. The framing that works best: the goal is outcomes, not process. Let's design the minimum governance that gives us confidence without creating more work than the program itself.

Most people, when you put it that way, agree. Not all of them. But most.

Programs don't fail because of bad engineers. They fail because of governance frameworks designed to demonstrate control rather than achieve outcomes. The difference is visible if you know where to look. And once you've seen it, you can't unsee it.

OKRs

Stop Writing OKRs You Can't Measure by Thursday

Shank Mahadev 7 min read

Every quarter, in organisations around the world, smart people gather in rooms — physical or virtual — and spend an astonishing amount of time writing objectives and key results that are, functionally, completely useless. They do this with great sincerity. The resulting documents are stored in Confluence, referenced in kickoff decks, and never looked at again until it's time to do the quarterly review, at which point everyone pretends they were tracking against them all along.

I've done this. I've watched it happen. I've sat in OKR review meetings where someone confidently presented progress against a key result that read "improve team collaboration" — which, I would like to note, is not a key result. It is a wish. A hope. A sentiment. You cannot put a number next to "improve team collaboration" and tell me whether you achieved it by Thursday.

Hence the rule I now use for everything: if you can't tell me whether you're on track by Thursday, it's not a key result.

The actual problem with most OKRs

Most key results measure activities, not outcomes. "Complete the migration to the new platform" is an activity. "Reduce P0/P1 incidents by 40% following migration" is an outcome. The first tells you someone did a thing. The second tells you whether the thing mattered.

The other failure mode — possibly more dangerous — is key results so ambitious they function as demoralisation engines. I have seen quarterly OKRs that would have required approximately four years and a different company to achieve. The team spent three weeks writing them, felt vaguely excited at the kickoff, and by week six had quietly stopped looking at them because the distance between where they were and where the OKR said they should be was psychologically crushing.

"An OKR that requires a quarterly review to evaluate is not a key result. It's a KPI wearing a costume."

What good looks like

A good key result has three properties:

  • It has a baseline. You know where you're starting from. "Reduce X by 40%" requires knowing what X currently is. Obvious? Apparently not — I have seen more OKRs without baselines than I care to count.
  • It moves weekly. You should be able to look at your key results at the end of every week and have a meaningful conversation about whether you're closer or further from the target. If nothing changes week to week, it's not a key result — it's a milestone.
  • The team can directly influence it. This one is underrated. Key results that depend entirely on factors outside the team's control are traps. The team will work hard, external conditions will change, and they'll either hit the number through luck or miss it through circumstances they couldn't affect either way. Neither is useful information.

The OKR conversation I now start every quarter with

Before writing a single OKR, I ask the team two questions. First: what are the two or three things that, if we got them right this quarter, we'd be genuinely proud? Not "completed" — proud. There's a difference. Second: how would we know, on any given Thursday, whether we're getting those things right?

The answer to the second question is your key result. If you can't answer it, you don't have a key result yet. You have an aspiration, which is a fine thing to have, but is not what you're trying to write.

OKRs are a tool, not a religion

The most dangerous thing that happened to OKRs is that they became a framework people worship rather than use. I've seen organisations spend more time debating the correct form of an OKR than doing the work the OKR was supposed to guide.

An OKR that drives a real decision this week is infinitely more valuable than a perfectly structured OKR that nobody consults until the end of quarter review. Format is a detail. Usefulness is the point.

Write fewer, better OKRs. Make them measurable. Give them baselines. Review them weekly. And if they're not driving decisions, change them — even mid-quarter. The framework is supposed to serve the work, not the other way around.

India Tech

Returning to India After a Decade: What's Changed for Engineering Leaders

Shank Mahadev 9 min read

I left India in my late twenties. Bengaluru was a different city then — faster than most places, but not yet in the mode it's in now. I spent the next decade and a bit in Australia, delivering programs for some of the country's largest companies, managing teams across six countries, and gradually accumulating what I can only describe as a very specific and slightly unusual set of skills.

When I came back, I expected to need time to adjust. What I didn't expect was to feel like I'd landed in a different country entirely. The talent, the ambition, the pace, the appetite for global-scale product thinking — it has genuinely changed. Not incrementally. Structurally.

Here's what I've observed, for what it's worth, from someone who has a foot in both worlds.

The talent pool is not what it was

The conventional wisdom among people who haven't spent time in India's tech ecosystem recently is that you come here for cost arbitrage. You offshore the repeatable work, keep strategy and leadership onshore, and manage the timezone gap as best you can. That model is obsolete. I'm not being polite — it is genuinely, structurally out of date.

The engineers I've worked with and interviewed back in Bengaluru are building global products, thinking in terms of scale and architecture, and are not remotely interested in being the execution arm of someone else's thinking. The best of them are leading product and engineering decisions, not receiving them. Any company that approaches India's talent market with the old mental model is going to lose, repeatedly, to the companies that don't.

"The engineers I've met back in Bengaluru are building global products. The old offshore mental model doesn't just undersell them — it actively repels them."

The ambition has changed

Something happened in the past decade that I can only partially explain from the outside: India's tech community stopped looking to leave and started looking to build. The reference points are different now. The aspiration isn't to move to San Francisco and join a startup there. It's to build the Indian company that San Francisco eventually has to pay attention to.

Zepto, Razorpay, Postman, Browserstack — these are not companies that happened despite being Indian. They're companies that happened because of where they were built and who built them. That shift in reference point changes everything. The ambition in the room when you're talking to a senior engineering leader in Bengaluru today is palpable in a way that, honestly, surprised me coming back.

What global experience actually brings

I want to be careful not to make this self-congratulatory, because that's not the point. But there is something specific that a decade of delivery experience across global programs brings that is genuinely valuable here, and I think it's worth naming clearly.

It's not technical superiority. It's not even process sophistication, necessarily. It's operating model thinking at scale. The experience of running programs across six countries simultaneously — managing stakeholders in multiple time zones, navigating different regulatory environments, building teams that can function without you in every room — produces a kind of instinct for what breaks at scale that is hard to develop without having been through it.

India's product engineering ecosystem is scaling fast. The companies that are going to need that kind of experience — and pay for it — are the ones growing from 50 to 500, from one market to five, from a single product to a platform. That growth is happening right now, here, faster than anywhere else I've seen.

What hasn't changed

The fundamentals of good delivery don't change by geography. Dependencies still need mapping. Stakeholders still need managing. Engineers still need clarity on priorities and space to do great work. Governance still needs to serve the outcome rather than protect the process. OKRs still need to be measurable. These things are as true in Bengaluru as they were in Brisbane.

What's different is the energy in the room when you talk about them. There is an appetite here — for rigor, for learning, for getting this right — that I find genuinely exciting. Not because India didn't have it before. But because right now, the conditions are in place for it to produce something remarkable.

I'm glad I came back. I'm more convinced every week that I came back at exactly the right time.
