A manual of sorts

Working
with Shank.

Most people call me Shank — not Shashank. If you're considering working with me, hiring me, or just want to know if we'd be a good fit, this page is for you.

I've spent 17 years delivering complex programs across three countries. I've managed teams, budgets, boards, and crises. I know what I bring and I know what I expect. Here's an honest manual.

What I value at work

  • Constant learning is non-negotiable. I live by it. I actively seek out people smarter than me — senior leaders, domain experts, anyone operating at a level I want to understand. If you stop growing, something has gone wrong.
  • High standards are a form of respect. I demand a lot from the people I work with. Not to be difficult — because I genuinely believe most people are capable of more than they're asked for.
  • Ownership goes all the way. If it's in your domain, own it completely. Don't wait to be chased. Don't bring a problem without having thought about the solution.
  • Directness over diplomacy. Say what you mean. I don't do office politics. People always know where they stand with me and I expect the same in return.
  • Progress beats perfection — usually. I lean towards perfectionism and I won't pretend otherwise. But I've learned to ask whether "better" is worth the cost of "later." Sometimes good enough, shipped on time, is the right call.
  • Data backs the decision. Gut feel has a role. But gut feel backed by evidence is better. I'll always ask for the numbers.
  • Optimism is a professional skill. Things go wrong in complex programs. Always. What separates good delivery leaders from great ones is staying clear-headed and forward-moving when they do.

How I operate

I'm hands-on. Even in senior leadership roles I've stayed close to the work — involved in architecture discussions, system design decisions, working through problems with tech leads and engineers directly. I don't manage from a distance.

I'm high energy. When I'm focused on a problem I work fast and I expect the people around me to match that pace. If something is blocked, I want to know immediately — not at the next standup, not end of day.

If I have to remind you of something we've already discussed, it's already too late. Timely follow-through is the baseline, not a bonus.

I invest heavily in people. Programs don't deliver themselves — people do. I'll spend real time understanding what makes someone tick, what's in the way, and what would help them do their best work. I've maintained 0% attrition through some of the most intense delivery periods of my career. That's not an accident.

I'm collaborative. I want the best idea to win, regardless of where it comes from. I have no ego about being wrong and I expect the same openness in return.

I'm calm under pressure. When things break — and in complex programs they always do — I don't spiral. I slow down, assess clearly, and move. The team needs that anchor and I take it seriously.

I question the status quo constantly. If something isn't working, we change it. I don't do "that's how we've always done it." And I love it when people around me do the same.

Low agency is a disease. If you're stuck, go around it. Talk to people. Find the answer. Don't sit and wait. Agency and urgency together are what move programs forward.

What you can expect from me

  • Full transparency on priorities, blockers, and where things stand. You'll never wonder what's going on.
  • Direct, detailed feedback — and a genuine willingness to discuss and debate it. If my feedback isn't clear, push back. I want us to land on the right answer together.
  • Protection of your focus. I'll do everything I can to make sure you're working on the right things, with clear priorities, without unnecessary noise.
  • Honest conversations early. I won't let problems fester. If something is off, I'll say it — and I expect the same from you.
  • Humour. Work is serious. It doesn't have to be sombre. Some of the best delivery moments I've been part of were also the ones with the most laughter.

Outside the work

My wife and son are the anchor for everything. The three of us have travelled to more countries together than most people visit in a lifetime — Fiji, Singapore, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Bali, Japan, Vietnam, Bhutan, and virtually all of Australia. We're nowhere near done.

Japan was the best of all of it. The culture, the food, the people, the quiet precision in everything — it changed how I think about craft. I'll go back many times.

I'm physical. The gym is non-negotiable — it's where I process and reset. Basketball, badminton, cricket, snooker. Competitive across all of them. I love the water and beaches — there's something about being next to something that large and indifferent that resets perspective completely.

Daily ritual
Cold water bath or ice plunge. Every single day. Not a trend — it genuinely works. It's how I start.
Always eating
I eat my way through every city I visit. Local spots, no menus in English. Japanese cuisine sits at the top of everything.
Tech curious
Genuinely fascinated by where things are going. AI in particular — not the hype, the actual capability and the gap between the two.
On repeat
Music is always on. Films are where I switch completely off. Both feed the same thing: appreciation for craft done well.
